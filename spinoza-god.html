Spinoza's God: Is religion illogical?


Baruch Spinoza was a Dutch philosopher of Portuguese Sephardi origin. One of the early thinkers of the Enlightenment and modern biblical criticism, including modern conceptions of the self and the universe, he came to be considered one of the great rationalists of 17th-century philosophy. On 27 July 1656, the Talmud Torah congregation of Amsterdam issued a writ of cherem (a kind of ban, expulsion) against the 23-year-old Spinoza. The primary reason behind this expulsion was his view about religion and God. Spinoza argued that God exists and is abstract and impersonal. He contended that everything that exists in Nature (i.e., everything in the Universe) is one Reality (substance) and there is only one set of rules governing the whole of the reality that surrounds us and of which we are part. Spinoza viewed God and Nature as two names for the same reality, namely a single, fundamental substance that is the basis of the universe and of which all lesser "entities" are actually modes or modifications, that all things are determined by Nature to exist and cause effects, and that the complex chain of cause-and-effect is understood only in part. His identification of God with nature was more fully explained in his posthumously published Ethics. Spinoza's main contention with Cartesian mind–body dualism was that, if mind and body were truly distinct, then it is not clear how they can coordinate in any manner. Humans presume themselves to have free will, he argues, which is a result of their awareness of appetites that affect their minds, while being unable to understand the reasons why they desire what they desire and act as they do. These ideas might be very normal for a 21st century person but we are talking about 17th century in Europe. This was the era of Church’s absolute superiority. Europe considered religion as important as it considers science today. This was also the last era of European Wars of Religion.


Thirty Years war had just ended and congress of Philadelphia had just occurred but it had not started to modify the world order yet. Church in Europe did not only exercise religious duties but also had immense political and judicial powers as well. But why are we discussing this man? Well, because this man had inspired the most brilliant scientific mind of human history i.e. Albert Einstein. Einstein’s religious views were heavily influenced by Spinoza.

We are in the position of a little child, entering a huge library whose walls are covered to the ceiling with books in many different tongues. The child knows that someone must have written those books. It does not know who or how. It does not understand the languages in which they are written. We see a universe marvelously arranged, obeying certain laws, but we understand the laws only dimly. Our limited minds cannot grasp the mysterious force that sways the constellations.
